Modeling Trail Visits

2022-05-27

This page shows our initial work with modeling counts of visits using information from the original trails dataset as well as weather data we merged in. The idea is to create predicted counts of trail visits in post-fire years that give us a sense of “what would visits have been like, given the weather and time of year, had the fire not happened.” Then, we can compare this baseline predicted estimate to what actually happened after the fire to get a sense of how the fire changed things.

Load in data

First, load in the trails data and the climate data from NOAA: https://www.ncdc.noaa.gov/cdo-web/. We pulled climate data from on the “CASCADE LOCKS, OR” weather station (GHCND:USC00351407). This is very close to the area of the trails.

setwd("C:/Users/stlp/Dropbox/Classes/SP 22/CSE 512/final")
v <- read.csv("monthly_vis_by_site_zeroed.csv")
# clean visit data to drop incorrect observation from 2016: 

v <- v[!(v$month==9 & v$year==2016 & v$years_fire==-2),]
c <- read.csv("climate.csv")

Clean climate data and extract relevant variables

c.a <- c %>%
  mutate(dstr = as.character(DATE)) %>%
  separate(dstr, into=c("year", "month"), sep="-") %>%
  mutate(month = as.numeric(month)) %>%
  select(year, month, TMIN, TMAX, SNOW, PRCP, TAVG)

Key variables in the climate data:

  • TMIN = average of daily minimum temperature across the month, in Fahrenheit
  • TMAX = average of daily maximum temperature across the month, in Fahrenheit
  • SNOW = total monthly snowfall (inches)
  • PRCP = total monthly precipitation (inches)
  • TAVG = average temperature across the month

Merge climate data to trails data

vc <- merge(v, c.a, by=c("month", "year"), all.x=TRUE)

Test out Poisson models for visit counts

First, create analytic dataset. We’re only using pre-fire data and will convert years and months to factors rather than estimating linear effects across month/year.

vc.a <- vc %>%
  filter(pre_post_fire=="pre") %>%
  mutate(year=as.factor(year),
         month = as.factor(month),
         Name = as.factor(Name))

First, fit a model with only: year indicators, month indicators, and trail indicators.

Higher average temperature is associated with an increase in predicted visits, while more precipitation is associated with fewer predicted visits. Predicted visits (relative to January) rise throughout the year, peak in June, then fall. Each year has seen more visits than the previous year. The model’s findings seem to align with what we gleamed in our A3 descriptive results.

Next, use this model to predict visits for three types of trails. We set the year to 2017 in post-fire years, assuming that 2018 visits would’ve most closely resembled 2017 visits.

Trail that stayed open and saw increase in visits after fire relative to pre-fire levels: Beacon Rock

beaconrock <- vc %>%
  filter(Name=="Beacon Rock") %>%
  select(year, month, Name, TAVG, PRCP, tot_vis) %>%
  mutate(monthchar = case_when(month < 10 ~ paste("0", as.character(month), sep=""),
                               month >=10 ~ as.character(month))) %>%
  mutate(ym = paste(as.character(year), monthchar,  sep="-")) %>%
  arrange(year, month) %>%
  mutate(year=as.factor(year),
         month = as.factor(month),
         Name = as.factor(Name))

beaconrock$year[beaconrock$year==2018 | beaconrock$year==2019] <- 2017
beaconrock$pred <- predict(m2, beaconrock, type="response")
brplot <- beaconrock %>%
  select(ym, tot_vis, pred) %>%
  pivot_longer(cols=c("tot_vis", "pred"), names_to="stat", values_to="val")

ggplot(brplot, aes(x=ym, y=val, col=stat, fill=stat)) + 
  geom_point() +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle=90)) + 
  ggtitle("Actual vs. Predicted Visits: Beacon Rock")
